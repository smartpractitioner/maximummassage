"Blending the 6-step process with keyword strategy" I agreed but let me expand and adjust our thinking on the "exact match is verbatim" rule in the header one as well on the hero. For Quality Score (the algorithm): No verbatim requirement exists. Landing page experience is scored on semantic relevance plus usability and transparency, not string matching. Google has no "H1 equals search term" bonus. A clear variation that means the same thing scores identically to a letter-for-letter match. There's no credible evidence otherwise, because Google doesn't expose QS at that granularity and its own guidance points to intent, not exact phrasing.
For conversions (the human): This is where the "it's what they searched" claim comes from, and it's real but misattributed. What lifts conversions is message match, and message match is a match of meaning, not spelling. A visitor recognizes "right place" the way you recognize a friend across a room. You don't need them wearing the identical outfit from last time. You recognize the face. "RMT in Calgary" and "registered massage therapist Calgary" read as the same face.
So the real target is exactly what you said: it reads naturally and unmistakably delivers what the query promised. The variable that actually moves the needle is the intent gap, not the string gap. Close the intent gap and you win. A headline about a different service than what they searched craters conversions. A natural rewording of the same service does not. Most case studies claiming "exact match made all the difference" are really showing message match working, and they conflate the semantic win with the literal string. Nobody has clean data proving a verbatim H1 beats a clear variation of the same intent.
Bottom line: aim for a headline that reads well and obviously matches what they wanted. We do want to make it a close match however.
